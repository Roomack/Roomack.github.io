Loops. As I walk the path to becoming a junior web developer, there are many concepts that are "must-haves" when it comes to coding, and "loops" are at the top of that list. They are our friends. They want to help us, but they need us to give them a beginning and even more importantly, an end.

A programming loop is used to execute a set of directions (code) over and over again, until a specific condition is met. This is absolutely essential when it comes to writing a dynamic program that can perform calculations and display output based on those calculations. Without loops, our programs would be much longer, complex, and limited in their functionality. Let's go over some of the different Ruby loop structures, as well as the ideal situations in which you would use them.

Until Loop

The cousin of the "while" loop is the "Until" loop. An until loop does just that - it continues to execute UNTIL a specific condition is met.




You can almost view an "Until" loop as a reverse "While" loop. They can often be used to achieve the same result, however your choice of which one to use may depend on your speecific program. Using a real-life example, if I were to instruct you to do your laundry, I could it in two different ways:

"Do your laundry as long as (WHILE) there are still dirty clothes left to be cleaned."

Or

"Do your laundry UNTIL there aren't any dirty clothes left."

See? It's the same task, with the same result, yet we've got two different ways to approach the instructions. WHILE & UNTIL - partners in crime!

Iterators

As with most things Ruby, this beautiful language provides us with multiple ways of achieving a result. Another method for running a piece of code over and over is to use iterators. An iterator is a method that functions similarly to a loop. A few example of iterators include:

The "loop" iterator:

loop {print "Froot Loops are TASTY!"}

This would then print that phrase an infinite number of times (we'll go over this later).


The ".each" iterator:

object.each {| element | do expression}

This is an iterator with greater power than the "loop" method in that it can be used to apply an expression to EACH element in an object.


The ".times"iterator:

20.times {puts "Bowl, spoon, and milk please!"}

This is essentially a more a concise version of a "For" loop. You would use this iterator when you know how many "TIMES" you want to execute a bit of code.Calling the method in this fashion would puts the string 20 times. Simple, right?
